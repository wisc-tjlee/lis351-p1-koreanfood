My name is Tracy, and I am a senior on the UW women's golf team studying biomedical engineering. For my hobbies, I love trying new foods and sharing about my experiences that are related to food.

Originally from South Korea, I am excited to be able to share about my heritage. Korea offers a vast and deeply-rooted culture that is expressed especially through avenues such as food. Although Korean pop music culture has gained popularity worldwide, Korean food also attracts the attention of travelers to the country.
There is so much to learn about Korea and its food culture!

Part of East Asia, colonial empirialism and contemporary wars had at one time characterised the country's struggles with poverty. However, Korean pop music and television entertainment has helped Korea rise to its recent prosperity.

Despite its tumultuous history, Korean food culture was not lost. Social media has also helped to people around the world to discover the wonders of Korean food through web content like "muk-bang". Muk-bang are videos of people eating large amounts of food, especially Korean food since this type of video content originated from Korea.

Out of the vast choices of Korean food, I will focus on three different types of dishes: an appetizer dish, a noodle dish, and a rice/soup dish.

해물파전 Hameul Pajeon
- The translation for this dish is "seafood pancake"
- This dish is a Korean style pancake that usually consists of seafood, green onions, and chili peppers
- Pronounced "hay-mool pah-jeon"
잡채 Japchae
- This is a dry noodle dish made of glass noodles
- Many variations of meat and vegetables can be added to modify the dish
- Pronounced "job-chae"
부대찌개 Budae Jjigae
- The translation for this dish is "army stew"
- This dish is an assortment of many ingredients into a stew and is a dish with a deep history
- Pronounced "boo-day gee-gae"
